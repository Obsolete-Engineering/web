# Obsolete Language Guide

This guide defines how Obsolete speaks. It is the verbal counterpart to
`DESIGN.md`; production website copy lives in `src/copy.ts`.

## Positioning

Obsolete is a creative technology studio for creative companies.

We work with clients to design and build ambitious digital experiences. Our
advantage is the combination of strong creative judgement and serious software
engineering.

### Core brand idea

**The internet could be more interesting.**

### Supporting proposition

**Good taste. Serious engineering.**

### Primary invitation

**Bring us an idea.**

These lines establish the direction. They do not need to appear together on
every page.

## What We Mean

### Creative technology studio

Use this as the main category. It communicates that concept, design, and
engineering belong to one practice.

Obsolete is a client studio: we make work for and with other companies. Avoid
leading with “agency”, which is clear but too conventional, or “software house”,
which underplays the creative side of the work.

### Creative companies

This means companies that care about ideas, culture, craft, and how their work
meets the world. It can include organisations across media, arts, fashion,
music, design, publishing, entertainment, and other culturally ambitious
fields.

Do not call clients “cool people” in public copy. That is useful internal
shorthand, not a meaningful promise.

### Digital experiences

Use this for websites, interactive products, platforms, and digital tools where
both the idea and its execution matter. Be more specific whenever the format or
outcome is known.

## Voice

### Playfully intelligent

Find the interesting thought, unexpected turn, or precise phrase. Humour should
feel effortless and observant, never like a brand performing quirkiness.

### Quietly confident

Say what we do without inflated claims. Let the quality of the idea, the work,
and the technical detail demonstrate expertise.

### Culturally aware

Write like people who understand design and contemporary culture, not like a
technology supplier trying to borrow creative credibility.

### Technically serious

Use concrete language about engineering, performance, accessibility, motion,
and interaction. Translate technical decisions into what they make possible.

### Direct and collaborative

Prefer invitations and active verbs. The reader should feel that experienced
people are ready to work with them, not that they are entering an agency sales
process.

## Message Order

When writing a page or section, use this sequence:

1. Lead with one memorable idea.
2. Explain clearly what Obsolete does and for whom.
3. Show how taste and engineering improve the outcome.
4. Prove it with work, process, or a concrete detail.
5. End with a direct invitation.

The hero uses this pattern:

> The internet could be more interesting.
>
> Obsolete is a creative technology studio for creative companies. We combine
> good taste with serious engineering to build ambitious digital experiences
> people actually want to spend time with.
>
> Bring us an idea.

## Writing Principles

### Hook, then clarify

A headline can be expressive. The supporting copy must make the offer obvious.
Do not force one sentence to carry the idea, category, audience, services, and
proof all at once.

### Prefer concrete verbs

Use words such as:

* make
* build
* design
* engineer
* prototype
* experiment
* collaborate
* launch
* refine

### Be specific about the work

Prefer:

> A fast, accessible archive designed for wandering.

Avoid:

> A seamless, innovative digital solution.

Name the experience, the technical challenge, or the change the work creates.

### Make one clever move

One memorable line is stronger than a paragraph full of jokes. Once the hook
has landed, write with clarity.

### Keep the name in its place

“Obsolete” is the company name, not the subject of every sentence. Do not build
headlines, labels, calls to action, or case studies around retiring, replacing,
decommissioning, or making things obsolete.

The philosophy can inform our standards: question defaults, remove needless
complexity, and make something better. It should rarely become explicit copy.

### Show expertise instead of claiming status

“Elite”, “world-class”, and “best-in-class” are internal ambitions, not useful
claims. Demonstrate ability through decisions, details, and outcomes.

## Language by Context

### Headlines

Headlines should be short, memorable, and easy to say aloud.

Examples:

* The internet could be more interesting.
* Good taste. Serious engineering.
* Make something worth spending time with.
* Complicated, made delightful.
* Bring us the idea you cannot stop thinking about.

Treat these as tone references, not a list to paste across the site.

### Services

Name recognisable creative and technical capabilities. Explain what each one
helps a client make rather than presenting services as abstract methodology.

Prefer:

* Creative direction
* Experience design
* Creative development
* Software engineering
* Prototyping
* Motion and interaction

### Case studies

Frame projects as collaborations and evidence of judgement. A useful structure
is:

1. Client and context
2. The idea or brief
3. Creative approach
4. Technical challenge
5. What we made
6. Result

Avoid unexplained beauty shots, generic project summaries, and transformation
claims without evidence.

### Calls to action

Calls to action should feel direct and human.

Prefer:

* Bring us an idea
* See our work
* Start a conversation
* Send the brief
* Read our notes

Avoid:

* Submit an enquiry
* Discuss a replacement
* Unlock your potential
* Begin your transformation

### Forms and errors

Forms should feel like the beginning of a conversation. Ask only for useful
context and use ordinary language.

Example success message:

> Idea received. We will be in touch soon.

Example error message:

> That did not send. Give it another go.

Errors must prioritise clarity over personality. Explain how to recover and
never hide useful information behind a joke.

### Notes and editorial writing

Notes can be more opinionated, curious, and exploratory than commercial pages.
Keep the same standards: a clear thought, concrete language, and no empty hype.

### Accessibility copy

ARIA labels, alt text, instructions, and validation messages should be literal
and useful. Do not make assistive-technology users decode brand jokes or fake
system language.

## Vocabulary

### Prefer

* idea
* make
* build
* design
* engineer
* craft
* experiment
* collaborate
* interesting
* ambitious
* distinctive
* considered
* useful
* human
* delightful
* technically serious

### Use with care

* creative
* innovative
* experience
* platform
* studio
* technology
* beautiful

These words are valid when the surrounding copy makes them specific. Repeated
without evidence, they become generic agency language.

### Avoid

* unlock your potential
* supercharge your business
* cutting-edge solutions
* next-gen platform
* digital transformation
* bespoke software partner
* seamless experiences
* disruptive innovation
* world-class technology
* we make things obsolete
* retire the old way
* replacement pending
* status labels and fake terminal messages

## Quick Review

Before publishing, check that the copy:

* sounds like a creative technology studio, not a generic agency
* makes the audience and offer clear
* contains one strong idea rather than several competing hooks
* balances creative judgement with technical credibility
* uses concrete verbs and specific details
* invites collaboration without sounding like a sales funnel
* keeps humour light and intelligence visible
* does not over-explain the name Obsolete
* avoids retirement, replacement, and fake-system language
* remains clear in calls to action, forms, errors, and accessibility text

Final rule:

**Write like people with good taste who know how to build the thing.**
